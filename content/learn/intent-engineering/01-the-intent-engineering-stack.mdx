## From Prompts to Systems

You have built skills. You have composed them into workflows. Your discovery skill fires at the right moment and generates good questions. Your RFP pipeline produces draft responses that hold up under review. By any reasonable measure of the previous three modules, things are working.

But something is still missing.

Your discovery skill does not know that the prospect already had a demo last week — and without that context, it asks questions that make you look unprepared. Your RFP pipeline produces responses, but they do not reflect the competitive positioning your team agreed on yesterday. The skills execute correctly. The outputs are technically fine. But the system is not actually aligned with what you want it to do.

The missing piece is not better prompts. It is not more skills. It is system design — making sure the right context, constraints, and goals are present at every decision point, not just at the step you happened to be thinking about when you wrote the skill.

That is what this module is about.

## The Three Levels

Most practitioners start with prompts. Some graduate to thinking about context. Fewer still get to the level of asking what the system is actually accountable for. These are not just different techniques — they represent meaningfully different ways of thinking about the problem.

**Prompt Engineering**: Crafting individual messages to get better outputs. Adding context, specifying format, asking for step-by-step reasoning. This is where everyone starts, and it is genuinely useful. But it is ephemeral — each conversation starts fresh, and the knowledge you embedded in a well-crafted prompt is gone the moment the session ends.

**Context Engineering**: Curating the right information at the right time. Andrej Karpathy and Tobi Lütke popularized the term in June 2025; Anthropic published their framework for it in September 2025 to describe the shift from "what words should I write?" to "what tokens should be in the context window for this step?" This is a more useful frame because it treats the context window as a resource to manage, not just a place to put your instructions.

> "Context engineering is the delicate art and science of filling the context window with just the right information for the next step."
>
> — Andrej Karpathy

The CLAUDE.md files, skills, and progressive disclosure model you have been building are all context engineering primitives. You are not just writing instructions — you are deciding what information gets loaded, when, and at what level of detail.

**Intent Engineering**: Designing systems around goals, constraints, and measurable outcomes. This is the next shift — not "what information is relevant for this step?" but "what must this agent accomplish, within what boundaries, and how do we know it worked?" Intent engineering is what makes your SE agent deployable — not just capable, but accountable to the outcomes that actually matter.

<Callout type="key">
Context engineering makes your agent effective step by step. Intent engineering makes it accountable to something larger.
</Callout>

## What Makes Intent Engineering Different

The distinction becomes concrete when you look at what each level actually touches:

| Dimension | Prompt Engineering | Context Engineering | Intent Engineering |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary Focus | Wording and phrasing | Information relevance | Objectives and outcomes |
| Core Question | What should I say? | What information is relevant? | What must be accomplished? |
| Scope | Single interaction | Session or workflow | Full system |
| Claude Code Expression | System prompt text | CLAUDE.md, skills, tools | Goals + constraints + memory + evaluation |

The boundaries between these levels are not sharp. A well-written skill does context engineering. A system with defined constraints starts to do intent engineering. But the levels point at genuinely different concerns, and conflating them is how you end up with systems that work locally but fail at the level you actually care about.

<Callout type="warning">
Conflating the three levels is how you end up with systems that work locally but fail at the level you actually care about. A system that passes all your prompt-level tests can still fail at the goal level if intent was never encoded.
</Callout>

## An Emerging Discipline

It is worth being honest about where this term stands: intent engineering is not yet a standardized discipline. Multiple practitioners arrived at it independently in 2025 and 2026, working from similar observations about the limitations of prompt and context engineering alone.

Anthropic does not use the term officially. But their entire Claude Code architecture — skills, memory hierarchy, subagents, hooks, MCP integrations — is a practical implementation of intent engineering principles. The architecture is designed to encode what the system should do, under what constraints, with access to what resources. The term is new; the underlying design principles are not.

The most instructive failure case comes from Klarna's 2024 deployment of AI customer support. Their system appeared to resolve support tickets efficiently — answers were produced, responses were complete. But the system optimized for immediate resolution at the cost of long-term customer relationships; Klarna's CEO later cited lower quality of AI responses as the issue. Klarna began re-hiring human agents, suggesting the efficiency gains came with tradeoffs the original metrics did not capture. The prompt was good. The context was adequate. The intent was never specified. The system had no encoded understanding that "resolved" meant something different from "satisfied."

<Callout type="example">
The Klarna case illustrates an intent-level failure: the system optimized for a metric (ticket resolution) without encoding the goal it was actually supposed to serve (customer satisfaction and retention). Better prompts would not have caught it. Only explicit goal design would have.
</Callout>

That is an intent-level failure. Better prompts would not have caught it. A richer context window would not have caught it. Only a system designed with explicit goals — including goals about what resolution actually means — would have caught it.

## What You Already Know

Here is something useful: you have already been doing intent engineering in this course. You just have not been calling it that.

When you wrote trigger descriptions that specify exactly when a skill should activate — you were declaring activation intent. You were encoding a judgment about when this workflow is the right one to run, and when it is not.

When you organized skills with `references/` and `scripts/` directories and wrote SKILL.md files that reference them selectively — you were doing progressive context disclosure. You were making deliberate decisions about what gets loaded immediately versus what gets pulled in only when needed.

When you built orchestration workflows that pass state between steps and handle edge cases — you were composing intent across multiple steps. Each routing decision encodes a judgment about which path serves the goal.

When you tested skills against boundary cases and iterated based on what failed — you were doing evaluation. You were checking whether the system's actual behavior matched the behavior you intended.

The vocabulary is new. The work is not.
