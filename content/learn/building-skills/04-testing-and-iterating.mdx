Building a skill is the first step. Trusting it is a different problem entirely. A skill that fires at the wrong time, produces generic output, or drifts out of scope isn't just useless — it erodes confidence in the whole system. This lesson gives you a systematic approach to testing before you deploy and iterating after you do.

## The Three Tests

Every skill needs three tests before you can rely on it in production work. Skip any of them and you're flying blind.

<Callout type="key">
The three tests — trigger, quality, and boundary — each catch a different failure mode. Skipping even one leaves a blind spot that will surface at the worst possible time: during real work with a real customer on the line.
</Callout>

### The Trigger Test

Does the skill fire on the right phrases — and, crucially, does it stay silent on unrelated ones?

Test three categories of input:

- **Exact phrases** from your trigger description
- **Paraphrases** — natural language variations a real user might type
- **Edge cases** — requests that are related but different enough that the skill should not fire

For a `discovery-questions` skill, that looks like:

- "prepare questions for my call with Acme" — should fire
- "help me prep for a technical discovery" — should fire
- "write a cold outreach email" — should NOT fire

**If the skill doesn't fire:** the trigger description is too specific or missing the phrases users naturally reach for. Add more quoted examples that reflect how people actually talk.

**If it fires on unrelated requests:** the trigger is too broad. Add specificity — phrases like "specifically for" or "in the context of" narrow the scope without sacrificing coverage.

### The Quality Test

Is the output good enough to send with minor edits? The bar isn't perfection. It's "better than starting from scratch."

Run the skill three times with meaningfully different inputs — different prospects, different industries, different deal stages. For each run, check:

- Does it follow the process steps in order?
- Does it use reference material when appropriate?
- Is the output specific to the input, or does it feel like it could apply to any customer?
- Would you need to rewrite it, or just tweak a few lines?

**If the output is generic:** your process steps are too vague. "Gather context" tells Claude almost nothing. "Gather prospect context: company name, industry, size, and current tech stack" produces something usable.

**If it misses key elements:** your process is incomplete. Add the missing steps explicitly — don't assume Claude will infer them.

**If output is inconsistent between runs:** your process has ambiguous steps. Make every instruction unambiguous. Describe the expected output format, not just the expected action.

<Callout type="tip">
Test on hard cases, not easy ones. A discovery call skill that works for a clean SaaS deal and fails on a messy financial services prospect with partial CRM data is not ready. The edge cases in your real pipeline — unusual industries, sparse information, multi-stakeholder calls — are exactly where a skill earns its keep or breaks down.
</Callout>

### The Boundary Test

Does the skill stay in its lane? A well-scoped skill does one thing completely rather than multiple things partially.

Watch for scope creep during your quality tests:

- The skill starts drafting follow-up emails when you asked it to prepare discovery questions
- It generates a full proposal when you asked it to parse RFP requirements
- It tries to update the CRM when you asked for a post-call summary

**If the skill overreaches:** tighten the process steps and add explicit boundary statements. A line like "This skill produces a structured question bank. It does NOT draft outreach emails or generate proposals" gives Claude a clear stopping point.

## Diagnosing Failures

Common failure modes have predictable causes and fixes.

**Doesn't fire at all.** The trigger description doesn't match what users actually type. Fix: add more quoted trigger phrases drawn from real queries in your daily work. If you wouldn't naturally say it that way, neither will a colleague.

**Fires on wrong requests.** The trigger is too broad. Fix: add specificity to the description. Narrow the language to the specific context where the skill belongs.

**Competing skills.** Two skills claim the same request. Fix: audit trigger descriptions for overlap and make them mutually exclusive. If there's genuine conceptual overlap, consider merging the skills or adding explicit boundary statements to each.

**Generic output.** Process steps are too high-level. Fix: add specificity to every step. Replace "research the company" with "identify the company's industry, size, primary products, and any recent news in the last 90 days."

**Inconsistent output.** Ambiguous steps produce different results each time Claude runs them. Fix: make every step unambiguous. If the step can be interpreted two ways, it will be — add an explicit output format description to remove the ambiguity.

<Details title="Diagnosing competing skill conflicts">
When two skills both claim the same trigger phrase, Claude has to pick one — and the choice may not be the one you intended. The fix is to audit trigger descriptions across all installed skills for semantic overlap, then make them mutually exclusive.

For example: if `discovery-questions` and `demo-prep` both respond to "prepare for my call," you have a conflict. The fix is to sharpen both triggers: `discovery-questions` should fire on phrases that emphasize learning about the prospect ("understand what they need," "find out their requirements"), while `demo-prep` should fire on phrases that emphasize building presentation material ("build a demo script," "prepare talking points").

If there is genuine functional overlap — the tasks are closely related and often done together — consider whether merging into one skill makes more sense than maintaining two competing ones. Two overlapping skills create cognitive overhead and unpredictable behavior; one well-scoped skill is easier to trust.
</Details>

## The Iteration Loop

Skills aren't write-once artifacts. The feedback loop that makes them production-ready:

1. **Use the skill** in real work for a week
2. **Note every friction point** — where did you override the output, edit heavily, or skip steps entirely?
3. **Update the skill** — bump the version, refine triggers, tighten process steps
4. **Test again** with the three tests before redeploying

Keep a simple friction log — three columns: What happened, Why it happened, What to change. After a week of use, group friction points by type (trigger misses, process gaps, output quality issues) and address each group in a version bump.

<Callout type="tip">
Version bumps create a history of what changed and when. Use semantic versioning: `0.1.0` to `0.2.0` for trigger changes, `0.1.0` to `0.1.1` for minor process tweaks, `0.x` to `1.0.0` when the skill is stable enough for team-wide use. When something breaks, the version history is how you trace what changed.
</Callout>

**Comparing versions.** When you bump from `0.1.0` to `0.2.0`, test both versions on the same three inputs. Compare outputs side-by-side. If the new version isn't clearly better on at least two of three inputs, the change isn't an improvement — revert.

## When to Split vs. Expand

Sometimes a skill grows too broad. Signs you need to split it:

- The SKILL.md body exceeds 2,000 words
- The process has meaningful branches ("if X, do this; if Y, do that")
- You find yourself skipping whole sections for certain use cases

Split by creating two focused skills from one broad one. "customer-communication" becomes "post-call-followup" and "cold-outreach" — each with their own triggers, processes, and reference material. Focused skills are easier to test and easier to trust.

Signs you should expand instead of split:

- The skill is under 800 words and output quality is still poor — remember, the target is 1,500–2,000 words. Expand first, then split only if the expanded version exceeds 2,000 words or develops branches.
- Users need to run two skills back-to-back that logically belong together
- The proposed split would produce skills with 90% overlapping content

When in doubt, keep the skill together and tighten what you have. Premature splitting creates maintenance overhead without improving quality.
